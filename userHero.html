    Am I Being Abused?

    A comprehensive guide to help you identify Abuse

    Introduction

    Abuse isn’t always easy to recognize. Many people imagine abuse as only physical violence, but it can take many forms—emotional, verbal, financial, sexual, and psychological. Sometimes, the signs are subtle and disguised as love, concern, or control. If you’ve ever asked yourself, “Am I being abused?”, that question alone is worth exploring with compassion and honesty.

    Image courtesy of Fauxels via Pexels

    Abuse is about power and control. It often leaves you feeling small, powerless, or unsafe. Here are some common forms:

    Emotional and Verbal Abuse

    Constant criticism, name-calling, or humiliation.

    Blaming you for everything that goes wrong.

    Using guilt or manipulation to control you.

    Gaslighting—making you doubt your own memories or feelings.

    Physical Abuse

    Hitting, slapping, pushing, or threatening violence.

    Restricting your movements, blocking exits, or intimidating with gestures.

    Destroying personal belongings to scare you.

    Sexual Abuse

    Forcing or pressuring you into sexual acts you don’t want.

    Ignoring your boundaries or dismissing consent.

    Using sex as a weapon, threat, or tool for control.

    Financial Abuse

    Taking control of all money and limiting your access.

    Preventing you from working or sabotaging your job.

    Using money to manipulate your choices or freedom.

    Psychological Abuse

    Isolating you from family, friends, or support systems.

    Monitoring your phone, messages, or social media.

    Threatening to harm themselves—or you—if you don’t comply.

    How Abuse Makes You Feel

    Abuse often leaves you questioning yourself. You might notice:

    • Feeling anxious, fearful, or “on edge” around your partner or family member.
    • Believing you are “not good enough” or that everything is your fault. Walking on eggshells to avoid conflict.
    • Feeling trapped, helpless, or worthless.

    If these feelings sound familiar, it’s important to remind yourself: it is not your fault. Abuse is a choice made by the abuser, not a reflection of your value or worth.

    What You Can Do

    Trust your instincts. If something feels wrong, it probably is.

    Reach out for support. Confide in a trusted friend, family member, or counselor.

    Know your resources. In many places, hotlines and shelters are available 24/7.

    Plan for safety. If you are in immediate danger, call emergency services.

    You Deserve Safety and Respect

    Asking “Am I being abused?” is a powerful and courageous first step. Abuse thrives in silence, but healing begins with awareness. No one deserves to be controlled, harmed, or diminished. You deserve love, safety, and respect.

    For years, I lived in silence. I experienced emotional and verbal abuse that slowly made me doubt my worth and question everything about myself. I felt trapped and alone, believing no one would understand me. One day, I decided to reach out for help. Through connecting with a compassionate counselor on this platform, I learned that what I was going through was not my fault. Together, we worked on healing, setting boundaries, and rebuilding my confidence. Today, I feel stronger. I still have difficult days, but I now know I’m not alone. Support is out there, and healing is possible. If you are reading this, please know that you, too, deserve safety, love, and peace.
